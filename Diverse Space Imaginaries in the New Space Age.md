# Diverse Space Imaginaries in the New Space Age

![](https://p.ipic.vip/j4rb9u.jpeg)
# Abstract

The New Space Age, shaped by the expansion of private aerospace initiatives, transnational collaborations, and renewed geopolitical and cultural debates surrounding outer space. Within this context, the article investigates how regional cultures, philosophical traditions, and technoscientific conditions on Earth shape artistic engagements with space. Drawing on comparative case studies associated with American frontier imaginaries, Russian Cosmism, Chinese philosophical traditions, Afrofuturism, Latin American decolonial perspectives, Indigenous cosmologies, and experimental practices across Europe, Canada, Australia, and Japan, the investigation demonstrates how Space Art articulates uneven power relations, resists colonial logics, and reconfigures planetary imaginaries. The article concludes by positioning Space Art as a valuable lens for cultural geography, through which spatial imaginaries, geopolitical asymmetries, and alternative futures beyond Earth can be critically examined. In doing so, it underscores the contribution of Space Art to understanding how different cultures negotiate technological futures and humanity’s changing relationship to the cosmos.

# Contribution

- Develops a cultural studies framework for investigating Space Art in the New Space Age through regional networks, philosophical cosmovisions, and political-technoscientific conditions.
    
- Demonstrates how diverse Space Art traditions—including American frontier imaginaries, Russian Cosmism, Chinese philosophical traditions, Afrofuturism, Indigenous cosmologies, and decolonial perspectives—produce distinct understandings of humanity’s relationship with outer space.
    
- Positions Space Art as a critical lens for examining cosmotechnics, decolonization, geopolitical asymmetries, and alternative futures in the New Space Age.
