# Human Bodies and Technology: The Reality and the Future of Reality

# Abstract

This paper examines the evolving relationship between human bodies and remote sensing technologies through anthropological, cybernetic, and posthuman perspectives. Focusing on satellite remote sensing, computational art, and Space Art, the study investigates three interconnected themes: the transition from biological adaptability to technological adaptability, the reconstruction of human subjectivity through remote sensing systems, and the emergence of posthuman neural politics. Drawing upon cybernetics, media theory, feminist technoscience, and environmental security studies, the paper argues that remote sensing technologies fundamentally reshape perception, cognition, and governance. Through these transformations, humanity increasingly exists within hybrid networks of biological, technological, and computational systems, requiring new conceptual frameworks for understanding reality and the future of human existence.

# Contribution

- Proposes a theoretical framework connecting remote sensing technology, cybernetics, and Space Art, addressing an epistemological gap between computational art practices and contemporary space-oriented technological infrastructures.
    
- Examines how remote sensing systems reconstruct human subjectivity by extending perception beyond biological limits and embedding human cognition within distributed sociotechnical assemblages of satellites, sensors, data infrastructures, and computational systems.
    
- Introduces the concept of posthuman neural politics in relation to satellite networks, cybernetic governance, and emerging human–machine symbiosis, highlighting the political and ethical implications of technologically mediated cognition.
