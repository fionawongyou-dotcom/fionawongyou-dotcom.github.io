# Da Wan Shi Ju: Interface Journey

# Exhibition Statement

_Interface Journey_ was the inaugural artist residency project initiated by Pingshan Art Museum. Taking the historic Hakka walled village of Dawan Shiju as its primary site, the project invited artists to engage in long-term field research, artistic residency, and site-specific creation, exploring how local history, architecture, social memory, technology, and urban transformation intersect within contemporary Shenzhen.

The exhibition proposes the concept of the "interface" as a method for understanding both place and contemporary existence. Natural environments, social structures, architectural spaces, digital networks, sensory experiences, language, and subjectivity can all be understood as interfaces through which individuals perceive, negotiate, and construct their relationship with the world. By traversing these overlapping interfaces, new forms of cultural understanding and artistic expression emerge.

Developed over three months of residency, research, and community engagement, the project brought together artists, scholars, anthropologists, cultural researchers, literary critics, and local participants. Rather than inserting spectacular objects into the historic site, the participating artists responded to the architecture, atmosphere, memories, and lived experiences embedded within Dawan Shiju. Their works became subtle interventions integrated into courtyards, walls, skylights, doorways, and everyday spaces, inviting visitors to move through multiple layers of time, perception, and meaning.

As part of Pingshan Art Museum's ongoing _Homeland_ series, _Interface Journey_ further investigates questions of locality, migration, memory, and identity within Shenzhen's rapidly transforming urban landscape. Through artistic residency as a methodology, the project explores how traditional cultural spaces may generate new possibilities for future-oriented artistic practice, creating dialogues between historical inheritance and contemporary imagination.

Rather than emphasizing spectacle, the exhibition advocates a reflective mode of public engagement. It asks how public art can create spaces for contemplation, how cultural transmission may occur through subtle encounters, and how artistic imagination can deepen our understanding of place, community, and the conditions of modern life.
