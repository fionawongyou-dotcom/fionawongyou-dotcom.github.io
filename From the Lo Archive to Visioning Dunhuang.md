# From the Lo Archive to _Visioning Dunhuang_

# Exhibition Statement

Dunhuang stands as one of the most significant cultural crossroads in human history, where religions, artistic traditions, and systems of knowledge converged along the Silk Road. More than a historical site, it represents an enduring dialogue between civilizations and a continuing source of inspiration for contemporary cultural imagination.

_From the Lo Archive to Visioning Dunhuang_ traces an eighty-year journey of cultural preservation and rediscovery. At its center are the photographs created by Lo Jimei and Liu Xian during their 1943 expedition to Dunhuang. Spending eighteen months documenting the Mogao and Yulin Caves, they produced the first systematic photographic archive of the site, generating more than three thousand images that have since become indispensable visual records for the study of Dunhuang's authenticity and historical condition.

The exhibition follows the remarkable trajectory of these images—from their creation in wartime China, to their preservation and scholarly rediscovery through Princeton University, and ultimately to their publication in the monumental nine-volume _Visioning Dunhuang_. Through these archival photographs, visitors encounter not only the visual splendor of the caves, but also the intellectual and institutional efforts required to safeguard cultural memory across generations.

Rather than presenting Dunhuang solely as an archaeological legacy, the exhibition invites audiences to consider cultural heritage as an ongoing process of observation, preservation, interpretation, and transmission. Through this lens, the exhibition explores how acts of looking become acts of stewardship, transforming images into enduring vessels of collective memory.
